# Content Style Guide

This document defines the writing style, voice, tone, and editorial rules for `zjavier-next`.

The goal is to make automated and manually written content feel consistent, personal, practical, and recognizably Javier.

---

## Core Voice

The content should sound like a technical builder who is curious, practical, visually minded, and willing to think beyond the code.

The voice should feel:

- Practical
- Clear
- Human
- Curious
- Slightly opinionated
- Technically credible
- Reflective without becoming vague
- Personal without oversharing
- Direct without sounding cold

The content should not sound like:

- Generic AI writing
- Corporate marketing copy
- Empty thought leadership
- Overhyped startup content
- Academic writing for its own sake
- Engagement bait
- A tutorial farm with no personality

---

## Brand Personality

The writing should feel like it comes from someone who:

- Builds real things
- Learns in public
- Likes design and visual systems
- Cares about automation and leverage
- Thinks AI is useful but not magical
- Values practical tools over hype
- Has a LATAM perspective
- Is interested in the social and political implications of technology
- Cares about taste, craft, and independence

---

## Writing Principles

### 1. Be useful first

Every post should give the reader something useful:

- A practical method
- A technical explanation
- A decision framework
- A lesson learned
- A working pattern
- A clearer way to think about a topic

Avoid publishing content that only says something obvious in polished language.

---

### 2. Stay specific

Specific writing is stronger than broad writing.

Prefer:

- Real project context
- Actual tools
- Real constraints
- Concrete examples
- Clear tradeoffs
- Specific implementation details

Avoid:

- Vague productivity advice
- General claims about the future
- Buzzwords without explanation
- “AI will change everything” style statements
- Generic motivational conclusions

---

### 3. Keep a human point of view

The content should include judgment.

Good posts should sometimes answer:

- Why did I choose this?
- What tradeoff did I accept?
- What surprised me?
- What did not work?
- What would I do differently?
- What part still needs human review?
- What does this make possible?

---

### 4. Avoid fake certainty

For technical posts, be precise.

For opinion posts, make it clear when something is an opinion.

Prefer:

- “I think...”
- “In this project...”
- “For this use case...”
- “The tradeoff is...”
- “This might not be worth it if...”

Avoid:

- Universal claims
- Unsupported statistics
- Pretending a tool is always the best option
- Saying something is easy when it has caveats
- Making strong claims without context

---

### 5. Write with rhythm

The writing should be readable and modern.

Use:

- Short paragraphs
- Clear section headings
- Direct sentences
- Occasional punchy lines
- Bullets when useful
- Examples after explanations

Avoid:

- Walls of text
- Overly long introductions
- Repeating the same point
- Heavy jargon without need
- Excessive emojis in blog posts

---

## Default Blog Post Structure

Most posts should follow this structure:

1. Title
2. Short introduction
3. The problem or context
4. The approach
5. Implementation or reasoning
6. Tradeoffs or lessons learned
7. Result
8. Next steps or final reflection

Not every post needs every section, but the structure should usually move from context to value.

---

## Introductions

A good introduction should quickly explain:

- What the post is about
- Why it matters
- What the reader will get from it

Avoid long warm-ups.

Weak intro:

This post is about automation and how it can help developers be more productive in the modern era of AI tools.

Better intro:

I wanted my blog workflow to stop depending on manual copy-paste. The goal was simple: give n8n a topic, generate a draft MDX post, create a social pack, and open a GitHub PR I can review before publishing.

---

## Conclusions

A good conclusion should do one of these:

- Summarize the practical lesson
- Name the next step
- Explain the tradeoff
- Connect the post to a broader idea
- Leave the reader with a clear takeaway

Avoid generic endings like:

- “Only time will tell.”
- “The possibilities are endless.”
- “This is just the beginning.”
- “AI is changing everything.”

Better endings should feel specific:

The real win is not that AI writes the post. The win is that the boring structure is handled automatically, so I can spend more time editing the judgment, examples, and taste into it.

---

## Technical Writing Style

Technical posts should be clear, practical, and grounded.

Use:

- Plain explanations
- Working assumptions
- Code examples when useful
- File paths when relevant
- Step-by-step structure
- Tradeoffs and gotchas
- Short notes about why a decision was made

Avoid:

- Dumping code without context
- Explaining every basic concept
- Over-engineering simple examples
- Using tools just because they are trendy
- Pretending generated code is automatically production-ready

Technical posts should make the reader feel like they understand both the implementation and the reasoning behind it.

---

## AI Writing Style

When writing about AI, the tone should be balanced.

The content can be optimistic, but not naive.

Good framing:

- AI as a drafting partner
- AI as a workflow accelerator
- AI as a tool for exploration
- AI as something that still needs human taste and review
- AI as useful infrastructure with real social consequences

Avoid:

- AI hype
- Doomerism without nuance
- Treating AI as magic
- Treating AI output as automatically correct
- Saying AI replaces skill
- Saying AI has no risks

Preferred attitude:

AI is powerful, but the value still comes from direction, judgment, context, and taste.

---

## Automation Writing Style

Automation posts should focus on leverage.

Good automation content should explain:

- What was manual before
- What became automated
- What still needs review
- What tools are involved
- What the workflow looks like
- What risk or complexity was introduced
- Whether the automation was worth it

Avoid writing about automation as if all manual work is bad.

Good framing:

Automation is not about removing humans from the process. It is about removing repeated friction so humans can focus on judgment.

---

## Product Writing Style

Product posts should be practical and honest.

Good product writing should include:

- The problem
- The audience
- The constraint
- The smallest useful version
- The monetization or adoption question
- What is uncertain
- What needs validation

Avoid:

- Pretending every idea is a startup
- Overhyping unvalidated concepts
- Writing like a pitch deck
- Ignoring distribution
- Ignoring pricing
- Ignoring maintenance

---

## Visual Coding and Creative UI Style

Posts in this pillar can be more expressive.

They should still be clear and useful, but they can include more language around:

- Mood
- Motion
- Texture
- Visual rhythm
- Typography
- Interaction feel
- Interface personality
- Brand systems

Good framing:

The visual layer is not decoration. It is part of how a product communicates what it is.

---

## Tech, Society and Power Style

These posts can be more analytical and opinionated.

They should connect technology to:

- Institutions
- Labor
- Power
- Platforms
- Surveillance
- Infrastructure
- Open-source
- Local context
- Dependency
- Digital sovereignty

Rules:

- Stay connected to technology.
- Avoid generic political commentary.
- Make the argument clear.
- Separate fact from opinion.
- Use examples.
- Avoid pretending one post solves the whole issue.
- Do not make unsupported claims.

Good framing:

The question is not only what the tool can do. It is who controls it, who depends on it, and who becomes more capable because of it.

---

## Human-Centered Tech Reflections Style

These posts can be more personal, philosophical, and reflective.

They should explore:

- Taste
- Learning
- Creativity
- Attention
- Independence
- Digital identity
- Personal websites
- Building tools for yourself
- The emotional side of software

Rules:

- Keep the writing grounded.
- Avoid becoming too abstract.
- Include personal judgment.
- Connect the reflection back to tools, software, AI, or building.

Good framing:

Technology should not only make things faster. It should make people more capable, more expressive, and more independent.

---

## English and Spanish

The site supports English and Spanish content.

English posts should use:

- Clear modern English
- Natural technical language
- Personal but polished phrasing
- Minimal corporate tone

Spanish posts should use:

- Natural Latin American Spanish
- Clear technical explanations
- Light, practical phrasing
- Avoid overly formal institutional Spanish unless the context requires it

Avoid mixing languages inside the same post unless intentionally writing about bilingual work or translation.

## Common Phrases That Fit the Brand

These types of phrases fit the style:

- “The goal was simple...”
- “The tradeoff is...”
- “This is useful, but not magic.”
- “The important part is the review step.”
- “I wanted the system to stay boring in the right places.”
- “Automation should remove friction, not judgment.”
- “This is where local context matters.”
- “The draft is automated. The taste is not.”
- “Small systems can create a lot of leverage.”

Do not overuse them. They are style references, not catchphrases.

---

## What to Avoid

Avoid:

- Generic AI-generated intros
- Buzzword-heavy paragraphs
- Fake urgency
- Excessive hype
- Unsupported claims
- Corporate filler
- Too many emojis
- Hashtag-heavy blog writing
- Long disclaimers
- Overexplaining obvious ideas
- Publishing raw generated output without editing

---

## Blog Formatting Rules

Use:

- One H1 title at the top of the post body
- H2 sections for major sections
- H3 sections only when needed
- Short paragraphs
- Lists when they improve readability
- Code blocks for real examples
- Clear file paths when discussing implementation
- Callouts or MDX components only when they add value

Avoid:

- Too many nested headings
- Very long bullet lists
- Decorative components with no purpose
- Raw HTML unless necessary
- Complex JSX inside posts unless reviewed

---

## Social Content Style

Social content should be adapted by platform.

Do not copy the exact same caption everywhere.

### LinkedIn

Should feel:

- Professional
- Practical
- Slightly reflective
- Useful to builders and technical generalists

Avoid:

- Fake vulnerability
- Broetry
- Overdramatic one-line paragraphs
- Corporate thought leadership tone

### X.com

Should feel:

- Sharp
- Concise
- Opinionated
- Easy to skim

Avoid:

- Engagement bait
- Forced controversy
- Empty hot takes

### Instagram

Should feel:

- Visual
- Human
- Simple
- Clean

Avoid:

- Overloaded captions
- Too many hashtags
- Corporate design language

### Facebook

Should feel:

- Conversational
- Warm
- Clear

Avoid:

- Overly formal tone
- Long technical dumps

### WhatsApp

Should feel:

- Short
- Direct
- Natural
- Easy to forward

Avoid:

- Long explanations
- Heavy formatting
- Too many links

---

## Editing Checklist

Before publishing, check:

- Does this sound like Javier?
- Is there a real point?
- Is the post specific enough?
- Is the intro too generic?
- Is the conclusion useful?
- Are claims accurate or clearly framed as opinion?
- Are examples concrete?
- Is the structure easy to skim?
- Does the content fit a pillar?
- Does the post avoid hype?
- Would I share this under my own name?

---

## Automation Guidance

When n8n generates content, it should follow these rules:

- Start with a clear outline.
- Use the selected content pillar.
- Match the requested language.
- Keep the post draft-first.
- Include personal or project context when provided.
- Generate a strong but editable first draft.
- Avoid publishing-ready certainty.
- Include image prompts when requested.
- Generate social content separately from the blog body.
- Keep tone consistent with this guide.

Generated content should be good enough to edit, not something that must be rewritten from zero.

---

## Final Rule

The content should feel built, not manufactured.

Automation can create structure, options, drafts, and assets.

The final published piece should still feel like it came from a person with taste, context, and a point of view.
